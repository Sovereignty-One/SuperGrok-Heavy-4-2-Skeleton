## PREAMBLE

The States Parties to this Convention,

RECOGNIZING the transformative potential and systemic risks of artificial intelligence systems,

## PART I ‚Äì DEFINITIONS AND SCOPE

### Article 1 ‚Äì Definitions

For the purposes of this Convention:

1. **‚ÄúAI System‚Äù** means any machine-based system that, for explicit or implicit objectives, infers from inputs how to generate outputs such as predictions, content, recommendations, or decisions that can influence physical or virtual environments.
1. **‚ÄúHigh-Risk AI System‚Äù** means an AI system identified as presenting significant potential for harm to health, safety, fundamental rights, the environment, democracy, or the rule of law, as defined in Appendix 2.
1. **‚ÄúQ-RAC Ledger‚Äù** (Quantum-Resistant Audit Chain) means a cryptographically secured, immutable, append-only log of AI system operations, decisions, and incidents, implementing post-quantum cryptographic standards.
1. **‚ÄúCertification Authority‚Äù** means a body accredited under this Convention to assess and certify AI systems for compliance with international standards.
1. **‚ÄúMutual Legal Assistance (MLA)‚Äù** means the framework for cross-border cooperation in AI system oversight, inspection, and enforcement.
1. **‚ÄúIncident‚Äù** means any AI system malfunction, security breach, rights violation, bias manifestation, or unexpected behavior meeting threshold criteria defined in Volume III.
1. **‚ÄúAudit Failure‚Äù** means:
- Failure to produce required Q-RAC logs
- Discovery of tampered or incomplete records
- Obstruction of MLA inspections
- Material misrepresentation in certification submissions
1. **‚ÄúSystemic Risk Event‚Äù** means:
- AI-caused harm affecting ‚â•1,000 individuals
- Cross-border cascading failures
- Critical infrastructure compromise
- Violation of fundamental rights at scale
- Deployment of uncertified AI in high-risk domains

## PART II ‚Äì CORE OBLIGATIONS

### Article 2 ‚Äì Continuous Monitoring and Logging

1. All AI systems SHALL implement continuous monitoring of outputs, decisions, and operations.
1. Monitoring data SHALL be recorded in Q-RAC ledgers with:
- Cryptographic integrity protection
- Tamper-evident mechanisms
- Granular timestamping
- Retention periods of minimum 7 years
1. Logs SHALL capture:
- Input-output pairs
- Decision pathways
- Confidence scores
- Human-override events
- Training data provenance
- Model version metadata
1. **Binding Requirement:** Quarterly reports SHALL automatically update certification scores via Volume IV metrics. Failure to submit reports triggers automatic status downgrade.

### Article 3 ‚Äì Post-Quantum Cryptographic Standards

1. All Q-RAC implementations SHALL use approved post-quantum algorithms:
- **Hashing:** SHA3-512, BLAKE3
- **Key Derivation:** Argon2id
- **Symmetric Encryption:** ChaCha20-Poly1305, AES-256-GCM
- **Digital Signatures:** Dilithium, Falcon, SPHINCS+
1. Algorithm agility SHALL be maintained to accommodate cryptographic updates.
1. Migration timelines for deprecated algorithms SHALL not exceed 18 months from announcement.

### Article 4 ‚Äì Incident Reporting and Root Cause Analysis

1. Incidents SHALL be flagged in Q-RAC within 24 hours of detection.
1. Root cause analysis SHALL be completed within 30 days, documenting:
- Causal chain analysis
- Affected populations
- Remediation measures
- Prevention mechanisms
1. Incidents meeting MLA thresholds SHALL trigger automatic cross-border notification.

### Article 5 ‚Äì International Certification Framework

1. AI systems SHALL obtain certification from recognized authorities demonstrating compliance with:
- ISO/IEC 42001 (AI Management Systems)
- ISO/IEC 23894 (Risk Management)
- IEEE 7000-series (Ethical AI)
- NIST AI Risk Management Framework
1. Certification SHALL be based on the scoring matrix in Appendix 1.
1. Certificates SHALL be:
- Publicly registered
- Cryptographically verifiable
- Subject to periodic re-assessment

### Article 6 ‚Äì Cross-Border Cooperation and MLA

1. States Parties SHALL establish MLA mechanisms enabling:
- Joint audits
- Log exchanges
- Incident investigations
- Enforcement cooperation
1. MLA requests SHALL be honored within 45 days unless substantial grounds for refusal exist.
1. Cross-border AI deployments SHALL undergo harmonized approval processes.

## PART III ‚Äì INSTITUTIONAL ENFORCEMENT & GOVERNANCE

### Article 7 ‚Äì UN AI Oversight & Enforcement Authority (UN-AIOEA)

1. A permanent **United Nations AI Oversight & Enforcement Authority (UN-AIOEA)** is hereby established as a specialized agency.
1. UN-AIOEA SHALL have authority to:
- Conduct compulsory inspections and audits
- Require production of Q-RAC ledgers and cryptographic proofs
- Interview technical personnel
- Access source code and training data under confidentiality protocols
- Impose graduated sanctions for non-compliance
1. **Sanctions MAY include:**
- Suspension or revocation of ISO/IEC-UN certification
- Prohibition of cross-border AI deployment
- UN procurement bans
- Financial penalties proportionate to violation severity
- Referral to the UN Security Council for systemic or willful violations
1. Enforcement actions SHALL be:
- Proportionate to violation severity
- Documented with detailed findings
- Subject to appeal under Article 8
- Published (with redactions for security/privacy) in the public registry
1. **Legal Basis:** UN Charter Articles 55‚Äì56 (international cooperation and compliance obligations).

### Article 8 ‚Äì Secretariat, Arbitration, and Judicial Review

#### Section 8.1 ‚Äì UNC-AI Secretariat

1. A **UNC-AI Secretariat** is established under UN-AIOEA administration.
1. The Secretariat SHALL:
- Maintain the **Global AI Certification Registry**
- Publish harmonized technical updates and guidance
- Coordinate MLA audits and inspections
- Provide technical assistance to developing nations
- Issue annual compliance reports
1. Registry SHALL contain:
- Certified AI systems with scores and status
- Certification authorities and their accreditation status
- Public incident summaries (privacy-protected)
- MLA cooperation metrics by jurisdiction

#### Section 8.2 ‚Äì Dispute Resolution

Disputes SHALL be resolved through a three-stage process:

**Stage 1: Technical Arbitration Panel**

- Composition: ISO/IEC experts, IEEE specialists, independent technical authorities
- Timeline: 90-day resolution target
- Scope: Technical compliance disputes, scoring disagreements, certification appeals

**Stage 2: UN-AIOEA Review**

- Appellate review of Panel decisions
- Authority to modify sanctions or remediation requirements
- Timeline: 60-day decision

**Stage 3: International Court of Justice (ICJ)**

- Final binding arbitration
- Reserved for:
  - Treaty interpretation disputes
  - Inter-state conflicts
  - Challenges to UN-AIOEA authority
- ICJ decisions are final and binding on all parties

### Article 9 ‚Äì Data Protection, Privacy & Human Rights Safeguards

#### Section 9.1 ‚Äì Privacy-Preserving Requirements

1. All monitoring, logging, and MLA exchanges SHALL be **privacy-preserving by default**.
1. **Mandatory safeguards:**
- **Cryptographic anonymization** or pseudonymization of personal data
- **Data minimization:** collection limited to purposes specified in this Convention
- **Purpose binding:** use restrictions enforceable through technical and legal controls
- **Zero-knowledge proofs** for cross-border verification where technically feasible
- **Differential privacy** techniques for aggregate reporting

#### Section 9.2 ‚Äì Disclosure Restrictions

1. Personal data MAY ONLY be disclosed when:
- Judicial authorization exists under applicable law
- Disclosure is strictly necessary for specified purposes
- Disclosure is logged in Q-RAC with purpose-binding metadata
- Affected individuals are notified unless prohibited by law

#### Section 9.3 ‚Äì International Standards Alignment

1. Compliance SHALL align with:
- **ICCPR Articles 17 & 19** (privacy and expression rights)
- **GDPR** principles (EU General Data Protection Regulation)
- **OECD Privacy Guidelines**
- **Council of Europe Convention 108+**
1. Where conflicts arise between this Convention and regional privacy laws, the **higher privacy standard** SHALL apply.

#### Section 9.4 ‚Äì Rights of Data Subjects

1. Individuals SHALL have rights to:
- Access AI decision records affecting them (subject to security exceptions)
- Request human review of automated decisions
- Seek correction of inaccurate data
- Lodge complaints with Data Protection Authorities
- Pursue judicial remedies for violations

- NIST SP 800-53 Rev. 5 (Security and Privacy Controls)

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This document provides a comprehensive mapping between UNC-AI-2026 requirements and NIST cybersecurity controls, enabling:

1. **Unified Compliance:** Organizations can satisfy both treaty and NIST obligations simultaneously
1. **Control Reuse:** Existing NIST implementations can be leveraged for UNC-AI-2026 compliance
1. **Audit Efficiency:** Single control assessment satisfies multiple frameworks
1. **Risk Management:** Integrated approach to AI system security and governance

-----

## MAPPING METHODOLOGY

### Mapping Categories

**DIRECT:** UNC-AI-2026 requirement directly corresponds to NIST control(s)  
**PARTIAL:** NIST control addresses part of UNC-AI-2026 requirement  
**ENHANCED:** UNC-AI-2026 requires additional measures beyond NIST baseline  
**NEW:** UNC-AI-2026 introduces requirement not covered by NIST

### Implementation Guidance

For each mapping:

- **UNC-AI Requirement:** Specific treaty article or provision
- **NIST Control(s):** Applicable control family and specific controls
- **Mapping Type:** DIRECT / PARTIAL / ENHANCED / NEW
- **Implementation Notes:** Practical guidance for unified compliance

## FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

### Q1: Is UNC-AI-2026 compatible with existing regulations (EU AI Act, China‚Äôs AI regulations)?

**A:** Yes. UNC-AI-2026 is designed as a **harmonization framework** that raises the floor, not replaces existing laws. The Model National Law includes provisions for ‚Äúhigher standard applies‚Äù in conflicts. The EU AI Act‚Äôs risk-based approach aligns with UNC-AI Appendix 2 categories.

-----

### Q2: What‚Äôs the cost of compliance for a typical AI company?

**A:** Highly variable based on:

- **Small startup (1-5 AI systems):** $50K-200K (mostly tooling + audit fees)
- **Mid-size company (5-50 systems):** $500K-2M (infrastructure + personnel)
- **Enterprise (50+ systems):** $5M-20M (comprehensive implementation)

**Cost reduction strategies:**

- Leverage existing NIST 800-53 investments (50-70% reuse)
- Use open-source Q-RAC implementations
- Phased rollout per implementation roadmap

-----

### Q3: How does this affect AI research and development?

**A:** Research exemptions exist (Model Law Section 3.3) for:

- Academic research with ethical approval
- Personal, non-commercial use
- Pre-deployment testing in sandboxes

Production deployment requires certification, but R&D is facilitated.

-----

### Q4: What happens to non-compliant AI systems?

**A:** Graduated enforcement:

1. **First violation:** Warning + 90-day remediation
1. **Continued violation:** Fines + operational restrictions
1. **Willful violation:** Certification revocation + UN procurement ban
1. **Systemic violation:** Security Council referral + comprehensive sanctions

Operators can appeal through Technical Panel ‚Üí UN-AIOEA ‚Üí ICJ.

-----

### Q5: Is this feasible for developing nations?

**A:** Yes. Support mechanisms include:

- **Technical assistance** from UN-AIOEA Secretariat
- **Capacity building** programs funded by developed nations
- **Grace periods** for implementation (12-24 months typical)
- **Open-source tools** reduce costs (Appendix C of NIST Mapping)
- **Tiered obligations** based on national AI maturity
